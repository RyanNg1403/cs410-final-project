02Lineage

When random noise stops scaling.

MAP-Elites is the search frame; deep RL is the source of high-quality candidates. The story of the last five years is the slowly-discovered cost of bolting them together. This page tells that story so the paper's solution makes sense.

2.1 The size problem

Picture a robot controller as a vector. Forty years ago that vector might have held a dozen numbers — joint gains, a few timing constants, perhaps a feedback offset. Hand-tuning that vector was reasonable. Searching it by random mutation was reasonable. And running pure MAP-Elites on it, the way Mouret & Clune did, produced beautiful results: a hexapod that learned to walk in dozens of distinct gaits, including ones that kept working after a leg was broken.

Modern neural-network policies are not those vectors. A small actor-critic for HalfCheetah has on the order of 10⁵ trainable parameters. Humanoid pushes towards 10⁶. The descriptor space remains low-dimensional — usually two or three — but the parameter space has exploded. And in that exploded space, most random perturbations of a good policy produce a terrible one.

Concretely, isoline variation is a partial fix: pulling offspring along the line between two known-good parents anchors them in a sensible direction. But it still relies on chance. To compete with end-to-end deep RL on raw fitness, MAP-Elites needs a way to actively improve a candidate policy with respect to the reward signal — not just roll a dice and check.

That is the opening that RL-augmented MAP-Elites methods walked through. The structure of every one of them is the same: keep MAP-Elites' archive and competition rule, but replace — or augment — the variation step with a few RL gradient updates. Three of these methods matter for the paper. Each adds capability; each adds a problem.

2.2 The lineage in three steps

Step one: gradient information from rollouts (ME-ES, 2020)

Colas et al. proposed updating each repertoire entry with Evolution-Strategies-style gradients. The recipe is unchanged at the algorithm level — sample a parent, mutate, evaluate, compete — except that "mutate" now means: perturb the parent with a cloud of Gaussian samples, evaluate them all, and take a smoothed gradient estimate that points towards higher fitness (or towards novelty, or a mix). ME-ES is a real advance over random mutation, but it pays in samples: each gradient step costs an entire batch of rollouts. The paper reports that ME-ES typically needs two orders of magnitude more environment interactions to reach the same fitness as gradient-based methods.

Step two: borrow the off-policy actor-critic (PGA-MAP-Elites, 2021)

Nilsson & Cully made the natural follow-up: skip ES, use a proper RL algorithm. They bolt the TD3 actor-critic onto MAP-Elites. Each iteration, half of the new offspring are produced by the isoline operator (the classical move) and the other half by running a few gradient steps of TD3 starting from a sampled archive parent. A single critic and a single replay buffer are shared across the entire population.

Step three: split the gradient into quality + diversity (QD-PG, 2022)

Pierrot et al. extend PGA-MAP-Elites with a per-timestep diversity reward — a signal at each environment step that encourages the trajectory to head into unexplored regions of descriptor space. QD-PG runs two critics (one for quality, one for diversity); half of the gradient updates use the quality critic, half the diversity critic. The result outperforms PGA-MAP-Elites on the harder, trap-style environments.

These three methods are the immediate ancestors. They make MAP-Elites work on deep-RL problems. They also inherit, between them, four distinct downsides that the paper addresses head-on.

2.3 What gets shared, and why it costs

Look closely at PGA-MAP-Elites and QD-PG and you find an architectural commitment that was not there in plain MAP-Elites: components shared across the population. Sharing was justified on sample-efficiency grounds. A single replay buffer pooling experience from all policies needs less wall-clock time to give the TD3 critic enough training signal. A single critic shared by all policies amortizes the cost of fitting it. The decision is reasonable. It is also, the paper argues, the root of several problems.

2.4 The four downsides

The paper enumerates them explicitly in its introduction. They are worth pinning to the wall, because the proposed solution is engineered specifically against each one.

Downside What it actually means Where it bites
(i) Hyperparameter sensitivity The TD3 (or SAC) inside is a fragile object — learning rate, exploration noise, target-update rate all need careful tuning. A setting that works on HalfCheetah is not the setting that works on HumanoidTrap. You spend a day tuning before each new environment.
(ii) Training instability RL is famously high-variance. Sharing a critic across hundreds of policies amplifies that: a bad batch on the critic poisons updates for everyone for the next many iterations. Two runs with the same hyperparameters can end up with archives that look like they were searched by different algorithms.
(iii) Low parallelism Shared replay buffer + shared critic = a serial bottleneck. You cannot put each policy's gradient updates on a separate accelerator. Wall-clock training time fails to scale even when GPUs are available.
(iv) RL-algorithm coupling The variation step is hard-wired to TD3 (PGA-MAP-Elites) or to TD3 + a diversity reward (QD-PG). If TD3 cannot solve your environment, neither can these methods. HumanoidTrap. TD3 famously cannot run Humanoid out-of-the-box; the original TD3 paper does not even attempt it.
The downside that hurts most

The first three downsides are engineering problems — annoying, sometimes expensive, but you can paper over them with compute and patience. The fourth is structural: it says your QD method cannot fix what TD3 cannot fix. The paper opens its abstract by flagging exactly this: "existing approaches mixing ME with RL tend to be tied to a specific RL algorithm, which effectively prevents their use on problems where the corresponding RL algorithm fails."

2.5 Where PBT comes in

Population-Based Training (Jaderberg et al., 2017) is the other ancestor the paper inherits from. It was invented for a completely different problem — finding good hyperparameters for a single RL agent — but its mechanics happen to be exactly what downsides (i) and (ii) need.

The PBT idea, briefly. You train not one agent but a population of, say, twenty agents in parallel, each with its own randomly-sampled hyperparameter setting. Every so often you rank them. The bottom p% of agents are replaced by copies of agents from the top n%. When an agent is replaced, its hyperparameters are perturbed or re-sampled. Training resumes. Repeat.

The trick to PBT is that hyperparameter search is no longer a separate, expensive, outer-loop wrapper around training. It's part of training. Strong hyperparameters propagate, weak ones get replaced, and the entire population sweeps the hyperparameter landscape while it sweeps the reward landscape.

That solves downsides (i) and (ii) at a stroke: you no longer hand-tune; instability gets smoothed by the population. And by training each agent independently — no shared critic, no shared buffer — PBT is embarrassingly parallel. Each member runs on its own accelerator. Downsides (i), (ii), (iii) addressed.

What PBT does not give you is diversity. It optimizes for the best single agent, with the population playing the role of a hyperparameter-search auxiliary. Coverage of behaviorally-distinct solutions is not on the agenda.

2.6 The synthesis the paper proposes

Two algorithms, each missing what the other supplies.

MAP-Elites (and its RL-augmented descendants) PBT
Maintains diversity yes — that's the entire point no
Adapts hyperparameters online no — they are fixed for the run yes — that's the entire point
Trivially parallel no for RL-augmented variants (shared critic) yes — agents train independently
Agnostic to inner RL algorithm no — coupled to TD3 in PGA-ME / QD-PG yes — wraps any RL algorithm as a black box

The paper's proposal is what you'd guess from the table: braid them together. Keep MAP-Elites' archive and competition rule. Replace the variation step with a PBT-style loop over a small population of full RL agents — each agent independent, each with its own hyperparameters that PBT-evolves over training. Use the inner RL algorithm of your choice (the paper tries both SAC and TD3). Insert the agents into the repertoire as usual.

Doing that cleanly requires one more change, which the paper makes carefully and which we will spend Part 03 unpacking: what gets evolved by the population is no longer just policy weights. It is the entire agent — policy and critic and replay buffer and hyperparameters — treated as one bundled object that PBT copies, mutates, and inserts. The bundling is what makes the algorithm agnostic to the inner RL choice; the per-agent independence is what makes it parallel; the PBT loop is what makes it robust to hyperparameters.

Pin this

PBT-MAP-Elites is not a new optimizer. It is the MAP-Elites loop you already know, plus the PBT loop you already know, with a precise glue layer between them. Part 03 shows what the glue actually looks like and why the result is more than the sum of its parts.
