01Primitives

One peak is not enough.

Before the paper introduces a new algorithm, it inherits an entire optimization philosophy. This page is for getting that philosophy under your skin — what Quality-Diversity is, why you need it, and how MAP-Elites makes it concrete.

1.1 The optimizer's reflex

Hand any optimizer a function and ask for the best input, and it will give you back a single number. Reinforcement learning trains a single policy to maximize a single scalar reward. SGD descends to a single minimum. Evolution Strategies returns a single winner. Across the whole machine-learning bestiary, the default verb is maximize and the default noun is solution, singular.

For a lot of problems that's the right reflex. For some, it is exactly the wrong one. A robot that can run forward isn't very useful if you also want it to back-pedal, side-step, or shuffle in a confined space. A controller that wins on a level surface may collapse on a slope. A policy that always sprints toward the goal isn't actually solving an environment where the direct path is blocked.

Quality-Diversity (QD) optimization is the family of methods that refuses the singular. Rather than returning the highest peak of the fitness landscape, a QD method returns a catalogue of locally-best solutions — one for every recognizably different way of solving the problem. It is, deliberately, a more expensive question to answer.

Working definition

A Quality-Diversity method trades sample-efficiency for coverage. Where standard optimization returns one high-fitness policy, a QD method maintains a collection of policies that are each high-fitness given a chosen behavioral niche. The collection is called a repertoire or archive; the niches are indexed by something called a behavior descriptor.

1.2 The trick: separate what from how

Every QD method makes the same load-bearing move at the start. It assumes you can cleanly separate two things about a policy: how good it is (a fitness scalar) and how it does what it does (a low-dimensional behavior descriptor). The first is the familiar scalar reward. The second is the design choice that makes the whole machinery work.

Formally we map every policy πθ through two functions. The fitness F: Θ → ℝ gives the scalar return — undiscounted episode reward, the same number you'd report to a reward-only optimizer. The descriptor Φ: Θ → 𝒟 gives a vector in some small, human-chosen behavior space 𝒟. Typical descriptor spaces are two- or three-dimensional. Typical descriptor functions are obtained by rolling the policy out and summarizing the trajectory.

Choosing the descriptor is the modelling decision. In a locomotion task you might pick the time-averaged frequency of each leg's ground contact: now an agent that hops is at one place in 𝒟, an agent that runs cleanly is at another, and a shuffler somewhere in between. In a trap-navigation task you might pick the (x, y) position of the robot at episode end. Now an agent that escapes north lives at one descriptor, an agent that escapes south at another, and an agent that gives up halfway and falls over lives somewhere near the start.

Descriptor design The paper's authors choose descriptors that are orthogonal to the fitness: position for goal-reaching tasks, foot-contact frequencies for unidirectional locomotion. The implicit theory is that diversity is most useful when it spans a dimension the fitness function does not already incentivize. Otherwise the search collapses back onto a single mode.

1.3 MAP-Elites: a grid you fill with elites

With F and Φ fixed, an algorithm needs to do two things: generate candidates, and decide what to keep. MAP-Elites (Mouret & Clune, 2015) is the simplest answer that actually works. It discretizes the descriptor space into a regular grid of cells, then enforces one simple invariant: each cell holds at most one policy — the highest-fitness one that has ever landed in it. That policy is called the cell's elite.

The loop, in plain pseudocode, takes about ten lines:

MAP-Elites (Mouret & Clune, 2015) initialize archive A ← empty map (cell → (π, F(π))) for g in 1..G: # 1. SELECT if A is empty: π ← sample random initial policy else: π ← sample uniformly from A's occupants # 2. VARY π' ← mutate(π) # add noise, isoline operator, etc. # 3. EVALUATE f ← F(π') # scalar fitness d ← Φ(π') # behavior descriptor # 4. COMPETE — the one-cell-one-elite invariant if cell(d) ∉ A or f > A[cell(d)].fitness: A[cell(d)] ← (π', f) return A

Everything interesting in QD happens either in the descriptor function Φ, in the variation operator mutate, or in the way candidates get scored. The skeleton above is invariant across decades of work.

1.4 The variation step, and why it matters

Vanilla MAP-Elites mutates a parent by adding Gaussian noise to its parameters. That works beautifully when policies are tiny (a few dozen continuous parameters describing a hexapod gait) and disastrously when policies are deep neural networks with hundreds of thousands of weights. The reason is the curse of dimensionality: most random perturbations in a very high-dimensional space point in useless directions, so the offspring's fitness collapses to zero before it has a chance to land in a new cell.

Modern QD methods replace pure noise with a smarter variation operator. The most common is the isoline operator (Vassiliades & Mouret, 2018), which samples a second parent from the archive and produces offspring distributed along the line connecting the two, plus a small isotropic noise:

θoff   =   θ1   +   Ν(0, σ12·I)   +   (θ2 − θ1) · Ν(0, σ22)

The isoline operator is doing something subtle. Each parent is itself a local optimum inside its descriptor cell. Their connecting line is a credible interpolation between two known-good policies. Offspring sampled along it are likelier to be reasonable than a random perturbation of either one alone. In practice this single change is what lets MAP-Elites scale to deep-network policies at all — and it's the operator the paper uses throughout.

1.6 Where this leaves us

Three ingredients, and a loop that fits on one slide. The behavior descriptor decides what counts as a niche. The variation operator decides how new candidates are proposed. The one-cell-one-elite rule decides what gets kept. Run that loop for a few hundred thousand iterations and you have an archive — a structured catalogue of high-performing, behaviorally distinct solutions. That archive is, end-to-end, the output of the algorithm.

Everything the paper does is downstream of this. It does not invent new descriptors. It does not invent a new variation operator. What it does is replace one specific sub-routine — the part that turns a sampled parent into an improved offspring — with something a lot more powerful than additive noise, and a lot more carefully managed than a single fixed RL training run. Take-home before part 02

MAP-Elites is a generic search frame. It will keep, score, and combine whatever you give it. The hard question is no longer "how do we search the space of solutions" — MAP-Elites answers that — but "how do we get high-quality candidates to feed it, when each candidate is a deep neural network that needs millions of environment steps to become useful?"
