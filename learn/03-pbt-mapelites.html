03The contribution

Evolve the agent, not the policy.

PBT-MAP-Elites is two algorithms you already know — MAP-Elites and Population-Based Training — locked together by one careful design decision. This page is about that decision, the algorithm it makes possible, and the result it delivers on the hardest environment in the benchmark.

3.1 The one design decision

Every algorithm in Part 02 evolved the same thing: a policy πθ. That meant just the actor's weights. Everything else — critic, replay buffer, hyperparameters — lived outside the variation operator. The critic and buffer were global; the hyperparameters were a user-tuned configuration that didn't change after launch.

PBT-MAP-Elites makes one change. It promotes the entire bundle of stateful pieces that define an RL agent into the object that gets evolved. An agent is no longer just πθ. An agent is a triple:

agent   =   (   θ  ,  φ  ,  h   )

The pieces:

  • θ — policy parameters. The actor's neural-network weights. The thing every previous method already evolved.
  • φ — other learnable parameters. Critic weights, target-network weights, optimizer state, replay buffer, anything that learns alongside the policy.
  • h — hyperparameters. Learning rate, exploration noise, discount factor, target-update rate. The dials that used to be fixed at launch.

It is the smallest possible-sounding change. It is also load-bearing for everything that follows. Bundling φ into the agent removes the shared-critic bottleneck — each agent now carries its own. Bundling h into the agent makes hyperparameters something the algorithm can evolve, not just a thing the user supplies. And once an agent is a single self-contained object, PBT can copy, mutate, and insert it without caring what the inner RL algorithm is. SAC, TD3, anything else — the outer loop doesn't look inside the bundle.

Why this fixes downside (iv)

PGA-MAP-Elites and QD-PG were tied to TD3 because TD3's critic and replay buffer were structural commitments of the outer algorithm — you couldn't swap TD3 for SAC without rewriting both. In PBT-MAP-Elites the inner RL algorithm is a function call on the agent bundle. Swapping SAC for TD3 changes only what's inside φ; the algorithm around it is identical. The paper exploits this directly: it reports results for both PBT-MAP-Elites (SAC) and PBT-MAP-Elites (TD3) and treats the comparison as informative, not as a confound.

3.2 Two loops, intertwined

With the agent defined, the algorithm proper is two interlocking loops. Both inherit from algorithms you already know, and neither does anything you haven't seen — but their composition is what the paper claims as novel.

The population update — who lives, who dies, what mutates

At the end of each training step, the P agents are ranked by fitness. Bottom p% agents (the paper sets p = 0.2) are deleted and replaced by clones of agents sampled from the top n% (n = 0.1). A middle band of k = 0.4 agents — neither top nor bottom — is replaced by random samples drawn from the MAP-Elites repertoire, not from the population. This is the cross-pollination between the two loops: agents that are good in their descriptor cell get a chance to compete in the population race.

Whenever an agent is replaced, its hyperparameters h are re-sampled from a user-specified range — not copied from the source. This is a subtle but important detail: the paper does not propagate strong hyperparameter values; it propagates strong policy weights and re-rolls the hyperparameter dice. The population is therefore continuously exploring the hyperparameter space alongside the reward landscape.

The repertoire update — variation + competition

After the population update, every agent runs S training steps of its inner RL algorithm (SAC or TD3 — your choice). Then a batch of agents is sampled from the repertoire and crossed with the population via the isoline operator on θ (the offspring inherit the parent's h, unchanged — this is the one place hyperparameters propagate). Each updated and offspring agent is then evaluated and competed into the repertoire by the usual one-cell-one-elite rule.

PBT-MAP-Elites — one outer iteration input: population P of agents (θ, φ, h); repertoire R # 1. POPULATION UPDATE — PBT-style rank agents in P by fitness replace bottom 20% ← copies of top 10% replace middle 40% ← random samples from repertoire R # crucial: re-sample h for every replaced agent for each replaced agent: h ← sample(hyperparameter ranges) # 2. INNER RL TRAINING — embarrassingly parallel for agent in P in parallel: agent.(θ, φ) ← RLstep(agent, S steps) # SAC or TD3 — black box # 3. REPERTOIRE UPDATE — MAP-Elites-style for agent in P: f, d ← F(agent.θ), Φ(agent.θ) compete into R[cell(d)] by fitness # offspring via isoline variation, also inserted sample M parents from R apply isoline operator to (θ, φ); offspring inherit h from one parent compete offspring into R

That's the entire algorithm. Both halves are off-the-shelf. The novelty is the bundling and the bookkeeping between them.

3.3 What this fixes, item by item

Map each of the four downsides from Part 02 against what PBT-MAP-Elites does. The symmetry is striking:

Downside How PBT-MAP-Elites addresses it
(i) Hyperparameter sensitivity Hyperparameters live inside the agent and are evolved by the population update. The user supplies a range, not a value.
(ii) Training instability Each agent carries its own critic and buffer. A bad gradient step poisons one agent, not the whole population. The PBT replacement step culls the failures.
(iii) Low parallelism Agents are independent objects with no shared state. Training each one is a job for a single accelerator. The paper measures near-linear speed-ups across machines.
(iv) RL-algorithm coupling The outer loop never inspects φ or the inner training procedure. SAC and TD3 both drop in; presumably anything else does too.

3.4 What hyperparameter evolution actually looks like

The paper's most visually striking result is not a number — it's a heat-map. After training PBT-MAP-Elites (TD3) on AntTrap for the full budget, the authors visualize the repertoire three times: once colored by fitness, once by the exploration-noise hyperparameter, once by the discount factor γ. They do this at four time points during training. The result is twelve panels showing the archive learning where to behave differently — and, separately, which hyperparameters to use in each behavioral region.

Why this matters

PGA-MAP-Elites and QD-PG run with one global setting of these hyperparameters. They have no way to say "use more noise here, less noise there". The fact that the optimal hyperparameter varies spatially across the descriptor space is, in retrospect, obvious — and exactly the kind of structure PBT was built to discover. The paper's claim is not "hyperparameters matter" (everyone knows that). It is "the right value of a hyperparameter is a function of where you are in behavior space, and the algorithm can learn that function for free."

3.5 The HumanoidTrap headline

The benchmark numbers are in §5 of the project page; we won't restage them here. There is one result worth pinning to the wall, however, because it lands the entire argument of the paper in a single observation.

HumanoidTrap is the hardest environment in the suite. A 17-DOF humanoid starts facing a wall; reward is forward velocity; the only way to actually make forward progress is to side-step around the obstacle. Running straight is locally optimal and globally terrible. Walking around it requires sustained, complex motor control and sustained exploration.

Method Solves HumanoidTrap within budget? Why / why not
MAP-Elites (vanilla) no Random mutation cannot drive a 17-DOF policy.
PGA-MAP-Elites (TD3) no TD3 itself cannot run vanilla Humanoid. The original TD3 paper doesn't try.
QD-PG (TD3) no Same reason: TD3-dependent.
ME-ES no Too sample-inefficient — it would need ~2 orders of magnitude more steps.
PBT alone no No diversity pressure; the population collapses onto the locally-optimal "run into the wall" policy.
PBT-MAP-Elites (SAC) yes SAC handles the inner Humanoid control; MAP-Elites' descriptor pressure forces sideways exploration; PBT keeps the hyperparameters in a useful regime throughout.

The point is not merely that PBT-MAP-Elites (SAC) wins. It is that every other baseline fails for a different reason, and PBT-MAP-Elites is the only method whose architecture allows it to address all of those failure modes at once. Diversity defeats the deceptive reward signal. SAC handles the control problem TD3 cannot. PBT keeps the hyperparameters appropriate as training progresses. Take away any one of the three and the result evaporates.

3.6 What to take away

Three things, in descending order of importance.

  1. The agent abstraction is the contribution. Once you decide that the unit of evolution is the bundle (θ, φ, h) rather than just θ, the rest is straightforward — both PBT and MAP-Elites are off-the-shelf. Most of the paper is checking that the straightforward thing actually works.
  2. Hyperparameters are functions of behavior, not constants. The twelve-panel figure is the most generalizable claim in the paper. Whenever you have a population that covers a behavior space, you should ask what hyperparameter setting each behavior wants. The answer is, very often, "a different one."
  3. Decoupling from the inner RL algorithm is what makes it useful. MAP-Elites variants that depend on a specific RL backbone have a ceiling: whatever their backbone can't do, they can't do either. PBT-MAP-Elites breaks that ceiling. The SAC vs TD3 comparison in the paper is a concrete demonstration. Anyone building on this work can substitute their preferred RL algorithm without rewriting the QD part.
